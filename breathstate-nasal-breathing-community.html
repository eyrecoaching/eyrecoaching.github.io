The Pattern

Most people were never
taught how to breathe well.

At rest. During sleep. Under stress. They don't know they're doing it. Quietly influencing sleep, recovery and airway health — and in children, facial development too.

James Nestor, Stanford University: Six days of enforced mouth breathing produced a 13-point rise in blood pressure, an HRV drop, and a 4,820% increase in snoring.

Mouth vs Nose

The airway matters

👄 Mouth Breathing
No filtration — allergens, bacteria and viruses enter unfiltered
Less nitric oxide exposure — less efficient oxygen uptake
Dries the airway — increases infection risk and disrupts sleep
May make it harder for the nervous system to fully settle
Disrupts sleep architecture — reduces deep sleep
Over time — may influence facial development and airway structure
vs
👃 Nasal Breathing
Filters, warms and humidifies every breath before it reaches the lungs
Produces nitric oxide — dilates blood vessels, may improve oxygen uptake efficiency
Maintains airway moisture — reduces snoring and infection risk
Activates parasympathetic system — signals safety to the nervous system
Supports deep sleep — the breath that supports recovery
Over years — maintains and supports healthy facial structure
Facial Structure

Breathing can shape
facial development

Dr John Mew's orthotropic research documented structural changes in identical twins — one nasal breather, one mouth breather. Breathing patterns may influence how the face and airway develop over time.

  • Longer, narrower face
  • Recessed jaw
  • Crowded teeth
  • Narrowed airway
  • Drooping eyelids and flattened cheekbones
📖

Mew, J. — Orthotropics: Identical twin studies show different facial development from breathing pattern alone. Same genetics. Different faces.

A
Mouth Breather Profile
Restricted Airway Development
Long, slack jaw. Open mouth at rest. Narrow palate. Crowded teeth. Eyes that appear drooping. Often misdiagnosed as simply needing orthodontics — breathing pattern may be part of the root cause.
B
Nasal Breather Profile
Balanced Facial Development
Wider palate. Straighter teeth. Defined jawline. Elevated cheekbones. Clearer airway. This is not genetics alone — it is the mechanical result of correct breathing pressure during development.

In adults the structural changes slow. The systemic effects may continue. Chronic mouth breathing during sleep may contribute to poorer sleep quality, elevated stress physiology, and less efficient oxygen delivery over time.

Kids
For Every Parent Here

Your child's airway and facial
structure are still developing.

A child's bones are still developing. Supporting nasal breathing early may help airway structure, sleep quality, facial development and learning capacity.

Bonuck et al, Pediatrics (2012): Sleep disordered breathing in children is associated with behavioural and neurodevelopmental problems — including symptoms often misattributed to ADHD.

Warning Signs

Is your child a
mouth breather?

These signs are common — but worth paying attention to.

Lips apart at rest
Snoring or noisy breathing at night
Restless sleep — tossing, waking frequently
Dark circles under the eyes
Difficulty concentrating at school — brain not recovering overnight
Crowded or misaligned teeth — narrow palate from mouth breathing
Frequent ear, nose or throat infections
What To Do Now

The correction
window is open

1
During the day
Gentle awareness
Lips together. Tongue on the roof of the mouth. Make it a game, not a correction. Children respond to modelling — breathe nasally yourself.
2
During sleep
Check the environment
Is the mouth open? Are they snoring? Address allergies and congestion — a blocked nose forces mouth breathing. Treat the cause.
3
Seek specialist input
Airway-focused practitioners
Myofunctional therapist. Airway-focused orthodontist. ENT for enlarged adenoids or tonsils. The window for structural correction narrows with age.
Nitric Oxide

What your nose
produces. Your mouth cannot.

Every nasal breath delivers nitric oxide — produced in the nasal sinuses. Mouth breathing produces none. Nasal breathing supports better oxygen uptake and nitric oxide availability.

🔬

Lundberg et al (1996): Nasal breathing produces significant nitric oxide in the nasal airways. Mouth breathing produces none. Foundational research confirmed across multiple studies since.

🔬

Nestor, J — Breath (2020): Nasal breathing supports more efficient oxygen uptake through nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation.

What Nitric Oxide Does
🩸
Dilates blood vessels
Widens arteries and capillaries — more blood flow to muscles and organs with every breath
💨
Supports oxygen uptake
Nasal breathing can improve oxygen uptake efficiency through nitric oxide availability
🛡️
Antiviral and antibacterial
Supports antimicrobial defence in the nasal airway
🧠
Supports brain blood flow
Increases cerebral blood flow — directly linked to cognitive performance and recall
❤️
Supports vascular tone
The vasodilating effect may support healthier blood pressure regulation with sustained nasal breathing practice
Sleep & Snoring

Snoring is common —
but it's not something to ignore.

!
What's happening
Airway obstruction
Mouth breathing during sleep can contribute to airway narrowing and tissue collapse. Sleep architecture fractures. Deep sleep diminishes.
→
What it may affect
Nightly disruption
Reduced oxygen saturation, lighter sleep, poorer recovery, morning fatigue, and — in some people — increased long-term cardiovascular strain. No amount of coffee fixes poor sleep.
✓
Tonight
What to do
Clear nasal passage before bed. Sleep on your side. Consider mouth tape — one strip across the centre of the lips. And do Flow 02 before sleep. The practice prepares the airway.

Mouth taping: used by some practitioners to encourage nasal breathing during sleep. Worth exploring carefully. A small piece of surgical tape across the lips. Start with the centre only. Build tolerance over a week. Not for everyone — if you have nasal obstruction or sleep apnoea, consult a physician first.

The Correction

Simple. Not easy. But simple.

1
Right now
Let the nose become the default
Lips together. Tongue on the roof of the mouth. Breathe through the nose. It may feel slightly more effortful at first. That's adaptation.
2
During exercise
Nasal only
It feels harder at first. That is your body adapting to more efficient oxygen delivery. Within 2–3 weeks exercise feels easier than it did breathing through your mouth. McKeown's Oxygen Advantage documents this in elite athletes.
3
Model it
For your children
Children copy what they see. If you breathe nasally — consistently, visibly — they will adopt the pattern. This may be one of the most useful things to notice for your child's airway development. Then show them.
📖

McKeown, P — The Oxygen Advantage: Nasal breathing during exercise improves performance, recovery and stress resilience. Within weeks of switching — measurable physiological improvement.
