Polyvagal Theory

Dr Stephen Porges.
1994.

The vagus nerve is the longest nerve in the body. It connects the brainstem directly to the heart, lungs and gut. 80% of its fibres are afferent — signals travel from body to brain. Your body is reporting to your brain about safety or threat before a single thought forms.

Breath is the fastest direct access point to this system. Breath is how you change your state.

🔬

Porges, S.W. (2011) — The Polyvagal Theory, Norton: Foundational framework for understanding how the autonomic nervous system regulates safety, threat and social connection.

The Three States

Where is your nervous system right now?

🟢
Ventral Vagal
Safe and Social
Connection · Curiosity · Creativity · Presence · Heart settled · Breath full and easy. This is where you do your best work and your best parenting. Most people visit this state — they don't live here.
🔴
Sympathetic
Fight or Flight
Hypervigilance · Urgency · Reactivity · Irritability · Heart racing · Breath shallow and fast. Everything feels urgent. Patience disappears. The state most modern professionals live in chronically — without knowing it.
🔵
Dorsal Vagal
Freeze and Collapse
Withdrawal · Numbness · Fog · Disconnection · Breath shallow and held · Body heavy. The state of shutdown. When the system has been in threat too long. Often misread as depression or laziness.

These are not personality types. They are not fixed. They are states — and the nervous system moves between them based on the cues it receives. Breath is the fastest cue you control.

The Tool

Physiological
Sigh

Stanford Huberman Lab, 2022. Tested against every other breathing technique for rapid anxiety reduction. The physiological sigh produced the fastest measurable reduction in stress markers of any technique tested.

The double inhale pops collapsed alveoli — maximising oxygen and CO₂ exchange. The long exhale activates the vagal brake — the fastest parasympathetic pathway available. Measurable state shift in 90 seconds.

Integration

One trigger. Seven days.

1
The Laptop Close
End of work
The moment the laptop closes. Four rounds. Non-negotiable. Don't leave your desk in fight mode. This is the boundary between work and the rest of your life — mark it with your breath.
2
The Red Light
In the car
One round. Just one. The car is one of the most private spaces you have all day. Every red light is an invitation. Nobody can see you. Nobody is watching. Use it.
3
The Driveway
Before you go in
Two rounds before you open the front door. Your family feels your state before you've said a single word. Give them the version of you that has actually left the day behind.

Choose one trigger. Not three. One. Attach the four rounds to it. Do it every day for seven days. The nervous system learns by repetition — not by intention.
